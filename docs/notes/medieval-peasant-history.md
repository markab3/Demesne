# Medieval Peasant System — Historical Research

Source: background research on medieval European peasant and labor systems.

This material informed the game's labor model (three-tier Peasants/Specialists/Retainers), the corvée mechanic, goods tax as render-in-kind, and the guild system's legal grounding.

---

## What Was a Peasant?

Peasant was a **legal and economic status**, not an occupational category. It described a person's subordinate tenure relationship to a lord — owing labor, rent in kind, or cash — not what work they actually did.

Internal gradations:
- **Serfs/villeins** — bound to the land, owed corvée and fees, could not leave without permission
- **Free peasants** — legally free, owed rent and sometimes labor, could move and sue in royal courts
- **Cottars, bordars, etc.** — sub-categories with smaller holdings and varying obligations

Agriculture was the norm simply because the medieval economy was overwhelmingly agricultural, but rural peasants also worked as smiths, millers, carpenters, fishermen, and charcoal burners. Skill did not change legal tenure status.

**Game design implication:** The game's peasant tier is defined by legal status and relationship to the lord, not by the specific tasks performed. The Specialist tier maps to the guild/urban legal distinction, not the occupation itself.

---

## Urban Escape Valve

Towns dissolved peasant status rather than containing it. A serf who fled to a town and remained for a year and a day was often considered free (*Stadtluft macht frei* — "city air makes you free"). Urban wage laborers, guild journeymen, and urban poor were functionally similar in economic precarity but legally distinct.

**Game design implication:** Specialists in the game occupy the urban/guild legal framework. The attraction mechanic (specialists arriving when conditions are right) loosely mirrors this — the city has to be worth migrating to.

---

## Paths Out of Peasant Status

Exit was difficult and rare in Early and High Middle Ages. Mechanisms:
- **Legal manumission** — a lord freed a serf by charter, at the lord's discretion
- **Purchase of freedom** — lump-sum payment to buy out obligations
- **Entering the clergy** — holy orders placed a person outside the feudal tenure system; lords sometimes blocked this
- **Royal military service** — direct service to the crown could elevate status; common soldiers were knighted after Crécy and Poitiers (1346, 1356)
- **Guild apprenticeship** — a peasant child sent to a town to apprentice entered a different legal framework over time
- **The Black Death (1340s)** — demographic collapse gave surviving peasants labor market leverage; serfdom effectively collapsed in Western Europe in the aftermath

## Guild Craftsmen

Guild-member craftsmen in chartered towns were **not peasants**. Rural craftsmen generally were, skill notwithstanding. The distinction was the urban/guild legal framework, not the occupation.

Product categories dominated by guilds in towns:
- **Textiles** — weaving, dyeing, fulling, tailoring; wool cloth the dominant export of England and Flanders
- **Metalwork** — blacksmiths, goldsmiths, silversmiths, armourers, bladesmiths; goldsmiths' guilds among the wealthiest in London and Paris
- **Leather goods** — cordwainers, tanners, saddlers (kept in separate guilds by production stage)
- **Bread and ale** — regulated via guild structures and municipal assizes (English Assize of Bread and Ale, 1266)
- **Construction trades** — masons, carpenters, glaziers

Guild control was local and urban. The same product made in the countryside sat outside guild jurisdiction — persistent tension with rural and foreign producers undercutting guild prices.

**Game design implication:** The guild system in-game mediates between craftsmen and the lord at the city level. Guild hall presence is required for collective bargaining and apprentice training — without it, specialists operate individually (rural craftsman mode).

## Peasant Miners and Foresters

Subsurface resources and forests were almost never owned by peasants. They were owned by the lord, crown, or Church. Peasant miners and woodcutters worked under an extractive arrangement structurally identical to agricultural serfdom.

### Mining

The entity taking a cut was whoever held the mineral rights — not necessarily the local manorial lord. Obligations could stack across multiple overlapping authorities simultaneously.

Notable legal frameworks:
- England: the crown claimed silver and gold deposits (*jus regale*)
- **Stannaries** of Cornwall/Devon: tin miners held distinct legal liberties under Stannary Courts, output taxed via *coinage* (duty on refined tin)
- Forest of Dean iron miners: similar ancient customary rights
- German silver mining: ***Bergrecht*** (mining law), a parallel legal framework

In most other cases peasants worked mines as direct labor obligations — essentially corvée underground.

**Key principle:** Favorable mining terms were granted not from generosity but because coerced unskilled labor produces skilled work badly. These arrangements tended to erode once mines were established and lords felt confident renegotiating.

**Game design implication:** The Miner specialist granting better extraction rates (vs. pure peasant corvée) is historically grounded. The Craft Rating system on mining reflects the skilled labor exception.

### Forestry

Royal forests in England operated under Forest Law. Peasants had no general right to fell timber. What they held were **common rights**:
- *Estover* — right to collect wood for fuel and building repair
- *Pannage* — right to graze pigs in forest

Organized timber felling required lord or crown license with peasant labor performing physical work under standard tenure obligations.

**Game design implication:** The Forester specialist managing sustainable timber without full deforestation reflects the common rights / managed harvest distinction. Clearing a tile entirely (removing the resource permanently) is the unmanaged alternative.

## Taxation in Kind and What Lords Did With It

Payment in kind (*render*) was standard before monetary economies matured: grain, livestock, eggs, cheese, ale, timber. Lords disposed of these goods through:

- **Direct consumption** — the lord's household (family, knights, retainers, servants, clergy) was a large consumption unit running on manorial renders
- **Itinerant lordship** — rather than transporting goods, lords and households moved between manors consuming each one's renders on-site (Charlemagne's court, English kings through the 12th century)
- **Feeding military retainers** — knights and men-at-arms in a lord's service were fed and housed from manorial renders
- **Sale at market** — as market towns developed from the 10th century, lords sold surplus renders; by the 13th century many English lords ran manors as deliberate commercial enterprises
- **Paying obligations upward** — lords owed obligations to their own superiors; commodity renders flowed up the hierarchy

**Game design implication:** The stockpile mechanic directly models this. Goods tax from peasant tiles deposits into the lord's stockpile. Retainers requisition from the stockpile. Surplus is sold. The flow is historically accurate.

### Commutation to Money Rents

From roughly the 12th century onward, renders in kind were gradually replaced by cash rents (*commutation*), driven by more reliable markets, more coin in circulation, and administrative convenience. By the 14th century cash rents dominated in much of Western Europe. The Black Death accelerated this.

**Game design implication:** The coinage production chain (Gold/Silver + Goldsmith → Coinage) granting a trade efficiency bonus citywide reflects this — monetary economies reduce transaction costs. This is a late-game refinement rather than a starting condition.
